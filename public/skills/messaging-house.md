# Messaging House

A skill for building a complete messaging architecture that the whole GTM team can use consistently.

## What this skill does

Creates a structured messaging house with:

1. Core promise (the one thing the company stands for)
2. Three supporting pillars (the proof points behind the promise)
3. Evidence for each pillar (data, customer stories, demonstrable capabilities)
4. Persona-specific variations (same promise, different framing per audience)
5. Channel-specific variations (web, sales, in-product, paid)
6. Test plan (what to validate before publishing)

## When to use

- Pre-launch messaging development
- Annual messaging refresh
- After a major product pivot or repositioning
- When sales and marketing are saying different things
- When the website is generic or feature-listy

## How to run this skill

### Step 1: Establish foundations

Ask for these inputs. If any are missing, flag them as blockers — you can't build messaging without positioning.

**Required:**
- Positioning statement (1 paragraph minimum)
- Primary persona (ideally a structured persona doc)
- Top 3 competitive alternatives
- 3-5 customer outcome examples

**Strongly recommended:**
- Closed-won customer quotes
- Win/loss patterns
- Product capabilities prioritized

**Helpful:**
- Existing messaging to react to (good or bad)
- Brand voice guidelines

If positioning is missing, stop and recommend the user define positioning first (suggest April Dunford's Obviously Awesome framework).

### Step 2: Draft the core promise

The core promise is one sentence. It must:

- Be a clear, specific outcome (not a feature or category)
- Pass the "10-year-old test" (a smart 10-year-old understands it)
- Be defensible (we can actually deliver on it)
- Be differentiated (a competitor couldn't make the same claim credibly)

Bad: "The unified platform for modern data teams."
Good: "Cut your data pipeline costs by 60% without rewriting a single query."

Draft 3 options. Force the user to pick one. Resist consensus mush.

### Step 3: Build three pillars

The pillars are the three capabilities or themes that make the core promise true. Each pillar should:

- Be a noun phrase or short clause
- Map to a real product capability
- Be distinct from the other two (no overlap)
- Cover the three most important objections or doubts a buyer would have

For each pillar, draft:
- Pillar name (2-5 words)
- One-sentence elaboration
- 3 proof points (quantitative + qualitative + demonstrable)

### Step 4: Add persona variations

For each major persona, write a tailored version of the same messaging house:

- **Economic buyer** — emphasize ROI, risk reduction, board-friendly framing
- **Champion** — emphasize career-making outcomes, internal narrative
- **End user** — emphasize ease, time savings, daily workflow improvement

Same core promise, different proof emphasis and language.

### Step 5: Generate the final messaging house

Use this structure:

```markdown
# Messaging House: [Product]
*Last updated: [Date]*
*Status: Draft / Tested / Live*

## Foundation
- Positioning: [Reference to positioning doc]
- Primary persona: [Reference to persona doc]
- Competitive alternatives: [Top 3]

---

## Core Promise
> [The one sentence]

**Why this promise:**
[1-2 sentences on why this is the right central claim — what it captures that competitors don't]

---

## Pillar 1: [Name]
**Elaboration:** [One sentence]

**Proof points:**
- Quantitative: [Stat or metric]
- Qualitative: [Customer quote or story]
- Demonstrable: [Feature or screenshot reference]

## Pillar 2: [Name]
[Same structure]

## Pillar 3: [Name]
[Same structure]

---

## Persona Variations

### For the Economic Buyer
- Core promise framing: [Adjusted version]
- Emphasis: [Which pillar to lead with]
- Key language shifts: [Replace X with Y]
- Avoid: [Words/concepts that backfire]

### For the Champion
[Same structure]

### For the End User
[Same structure]

---

## Channel Variations

### Website hero
[Headline + subhead]

### Sales pitch opener
[30-second elevator pitch]

### Paid ad copy
[3 short variants]

### In-product messaging
[Onboarding hook, upgrade prompt]

---

## What we will NOT say
- [Anti-claims that competitors make that we deliberately avoid]
- [Words we don't use]
- [Comparisons we don't make]

---

## Test plan
- Wynter test: [Which messages to test, against which audience]
- Internal validation: [Who reviews before publishing]
- Win/loss check: [Tracking how messaging shows up in calls]
- Iteration cadence: [When we revisit]
```

### Step 6: Recommend testing

Always end by recommending the user test the top messages before publishing. Specifically:

- Hero headline candidates → Wynter or similar
- Sales opener → role-play with 3 reps before rolling out
- Pillars → review with a customer or two

Never let messaging go live without external validation. Internal consensus is a trap.

## What good output looks like

- Specific, outcome-focused promise — not "the best platform for X"
- Pillars that are distinct, not three ways of saying the same thing
- Proof points that include real numbers and real customer quotes
- Persona variations that change meaningfully, not just word swaps
- Anti-claims that show what we deliberately don't say

## What to avoid

- Vague aspirational language ("empower," "unleash," "transform")
- Pillars that are features ("AI-powered analytics" is a feature, not a pillar)
- Three pillars that all describe ease of use (no differentiation between them)
- Skipping the testing recommendation
- Treating this as a one-time output. Messaging is iterated, not finished.
