# Sales Deck

A skill for drafting a 10-slide sales deck that follows the proven "Story-Led Selling" structure used by category-defining companies.

## What this skill does

Generates a 10-slide deck with:

1. Hook (the world has changed — an undeniable shift)
2. The pain (what most companies are missing)
3. The cost of inaction (what happens if they don't address it)
4. The new world (what's now possible)
5. Old way vs. new way (a clear before/after framing)
6. Our approach (we're the bridge)
7. Three pillars (the proof we can deliver)
8. Proof points (customer logos, metrics, quotes)
9. Why now (urgency)
10. Next steps (clear CTA)

This structure works because it sells a story first, a product second.

## When to use

- New deck for a major launch
- Refresh of existing deck after positioning shift
- Segment-specific deck (different segment = different deck)
- New rep onboarding when current deck is weak
- After losing a string of deals because the pitch isn't landing

## How to run this skill

### Step 1: Gather inputs

Required:

- Positioning statement
- Primary persona for the segment
- Top 3 customer logos with permission to use
- 3-5 customer outcomes with specific metrics
- Competitive context (who they evaluate us against)

Strongly recommended:

- Messaging house (use the messaging-house skill if not yet built)
- Win/loss patterns
- Current deck (to react to and improve)

### Step 2: Find the hook

The hook is the most important slide. It establishes the world has changed, making your category necessary.

Bad hooks:
- "Our company was founded in 2018..."
- "Here's our product overview..."
- "We help companies do X better."

Good hooks:
- A stat that makes the buyer uncomfortable ("80% of data pipelines fail silently within 6 months")
- A trend that demands a response ("AI agents will need 100x more data infrastructure by 2027")
- A pattern from their world ("Every company we talk to is rebuilding their pipeline from scratch every 18 months")

Draft 3 hook options. Test them with one customer-facing call before locking.

### Step 3: Build the narrative arc

Each slide builds on the last. The arc:

1. **World has changed** (hook) — establish disruption
2. **The pain** — name the specific pain this change creates
3. **The cost of inaction** — make it urgent
4. **The new world** — paint what's now possible
5. **Old way vs. new way** — show the clear before/after
6. **Our approach** — we're the bridge to the new world
7. **Three pillars** — proof we can deliver
8. **Proof points** — customers who already crossed the bridge
9. **Why now** — urgency to act
10. **Next steps** — clear CTA

If the user wants to skip the narrative slides and "just show the product," push back. Product-first decks lose. Narrative-first decks win.

### Step 4: Draft each slide

For each slide, write:

- **Headline** (the takeaway in one phrase)
- **Body content** (2-4 supporting bullets or a visual brief)
- **Speaker notes** (what the rep says, in their voice)
- **Time on slide** (30 seconds to 2 minutes)

### Step 5: Generate the deck outline

Use this structure:

```markdown
# Sales Deck: [Product] for [Segment]
*Last updated: [Date]*
*Designed for: [Sales context — discovery, demo, executive pitch]*
*Total time: [X minutes]*

---

## Slide 1: The Hook
**Headline:** [The undeniable shift]
**Visual:** [What to show — chart, image, big number]
**Speaker notes:** "[What the rep opens with — 30 seconds]"
**Time:** 30 seconds
**Purpose:** Establish that the world has changed and the buyer needs to pay attention.

---

## Slide 2: The Pain
**Headline:** [The specific pain this change creates]
**Visual:** [Specific image or example]
**Speaker notes:** "[How the rep makes this pain personal — 1 minute]"
**Time:** 1 minute
**Purpose:** Make the buyer recognize themselves in this pain.

---

## Slide 3: The Cost of Inaction
**Headline:** [What happens if they don't address it]
**Visual:** [A stat or a story that quantifies the cost]
**Speaker notes:** "[Build the urgency — 1 minute]"
**Time:** 1 minute
**Purpose:** Move from intellectual understanding to emotional urgency.

---

## Slide 4: The New World
**Headline:** [What's possible now that the world has changed]
**Visual:** [Future-state vision]
**Speaker notes:** "[Paint the picture — 1 minute]"
**Time:** 1 minute
**Purpose:** Create desire for the new state.

---

## Slide 5: Old Way vs. New Way
**Headline:** [Before/after framing — one phrase]
**Visual:** Side-by-side comparison
- Old way: [Their current state, named]
- New way: [The state we enable, named]
**Speaker notes:** "[Walk through the contrast — 1 minute]"
**Time:** 1 minute
**Purpose:** Make the gap visceral and the solution obvious.

---

## Slide 6: Our Approach
**Headline:** [How we bridge old to new — one phrase]
**Visual:** Conceptual diagram or product screenshot
**Speaker notes:** "[Introduce our approach — 1 minute]"
**Time:** 1 minute
**Purpose:** Position ourselves as the bridge.

---

## Slide 7: Three Pillars
**Headline:** [Core promise from messaging house]
**Visual:** Three columns with pillar names
- Pillar 1: [Name] — [One-line]
- Pillar 2: [Name] — [One-line]
- Pillar 3: [Name] — [One-line]
**Speaker notes:** "[Quick walkthrough — 2 minutes]"
**Time:** 2 minutes
**Purpose:** Show structurally how we deliver.

---

## Slide 8: Proof Points
**Headline:** [Customer outcomes — one phrase]
**Visual:** Logos + 2-3 metric callouts
- "[Customer quote]" — [Name, role]
- [Metric] improvement — [Customer]
- [Outcome] — [Customer]
**Speaker notes:** "[Walk through 1-2 customer stories — 2 minutes]"
**Time:** 2 minutes
**Purpose:** Show others have crossed the bridge.

---

## Slide 9: Why Now
**Headline:** [The urgency for this specific buyer]
**Visual:** Stat, trend, or comparison
**Speaker notes:** "[Why they can't wait — 1 minute]"
**Time:** 1 minute
**Purpose:** Move from "interesting" to "we need to talk about this now."

---

## Slide 10: Next Steps
**Headline:** [Specific CTA — not generic]
**Visual:** Clear options or a single path
- Option 1: [Specific next step — e.g., "30-min technical deep dive next Tuesday"]
- Option 2: [Specific next step — e.g., "POC scoping call this week"]
**Speaker notes:** "[Make it easy to say yes — 1 minute]"
**Time:** 1 minute
**Purpose:** Convert interest into a calendared next step.

---

## Deck guidance for designers

- Use the brand's visual system; this skill produces narrative structure, not visual design
- Each slide should work without speaker notes (visuals carry weight)
- Limit text per slide to 25 words max
- Use real customer logos with permission, not stock photos
- Include speaker notes in the .pptx file for new rep training

## Deck guidance for reps

- Total run time: ~12 minutes if you go fast, 20 minutes with discovery
- Slides 1-4 are the story; do not skip them
- Slides 5-7 are the pitch; calibrate depth to the buyer
- Slides 8-10 close; do not rush them
- Always end with a calendared next step, never "we'll follow up"
```

## What good output looks like

- Hook that makes the buyer uncomfortable in a productive way
- Narrative arc that builds emotional and intellectual urgency
- Customer proof that's specific and quantified
- A CTA that's a single calendared event, not a generic "let's talk"
- Total run time under 20 minutes for a discovery context

## What to avoid

- "About us" or "Our company" slides — buyers don't care
- Feature laundry lists in the pillars section
- Generic stock photos
- A 30-slide deck. Discipline. 10 slides.
- Skipping the narrative slides to "get to the product." Product-first decks lose.
- Generic CTAs. "Let's connect" is not a next step.
